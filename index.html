About Ontologies ▲

An ontology, in the philosophical sense, is a theory of what exists — an account of the fundamental categories of being. The word comes from the Greek ontos (being) and logos (study). Where empirical sciences ask what kinds of things inhabit a domain, ontology asks what kinds of things are even possible — what basic types reality must contain in order for anything else to be the case.

Categorial schemes

Every ontology in this collection is specifically a categorial scheme: a small inventory of highest-level genera such as substance, universal, particular, or process. The idea, inherited from Aristotle's Categories, is that these genera are not reducible to one another and that every other kind of thing belongs under at least one of them. Different schemes disagree sharply on which categories to posit and on whether the scheme itself is mind-independent or partly shaped by cognition.

Three broad stances

Mind-independent ontologies (Plato, Aristotle, BFO, SUMO …) hold that categories carve reality at its actual joints, independently of any knower. Mind-inclusive ones (DOLCE, Husserl, Meinong …) accept intentional, fictional, or social entities as full members of the ontology. Representational schemes (EFO) treat the ontology as a structured representation useful for reasoning, without strong commitments about what ultimately exists. Radical Constructivism (Glasersfeld) is a meta-position that rejects all three framings.

Formal vs. philosophical

Since the early 2000s, several ontologies have been given machine-readable encodings in OWL 2, Common Logic, or KIF — making them amenable to automated reasoning, data integration, and semantic web applications. Philosophical schemes (Plato through Husserl) remain purely textual; their categories must be interpreted and operationalised before they can inform any formal system.
